My Political Views

Published: 2017-02-11

An attempt to succinctly state my political beliefs and conclusions. My beliefs will be inherently arbitrary, and my conclusions inevitably based on incomplete or false knowledge – however we are all political beings, and should at the very least be held responsible to understand ourselves as such.

What I mean by “belief” and “conclusion” is explained here.

If I were pressed to categorize myself as holding some political creed, I would guess I am something like a libertarian socialist, though I certainly part ways here when it comes to their notion that abolishing the state altogether is somehow desirable (even if it were a possibility that exists anywhere outside the pipe-dreams of anarchists).

The political theorists who have influenced me most are John Stuart Mill, Noam Chomsky, and Hannah Arendt.

Of particular interest to me at the moment is the Rojava experiment, and the post-incarceration writings of Abdullah Öcalan on his political theory of democratic confederalism. His short book on the matter is available as a free PDF here.

(Whether the Öcalan and the Rojava experiment will succeed in their putative goals, and the sincerity of those goals, remains to be seen; but one must admire the extent to which this vision is being put to practice; and the writings are refreshing.)

I’m a socialist in the sense that I believe any defensible political system must strive to minimize excessive suffering and coercion and provide some level of comfort given the resources that are available to it as a whole.

I’m a staunch individualist in the sense that I believe that the ideal political systems are those which allows the individual to flourish, by his own means, and to experience life by his own terms.

From this stems my complete rejection of not only right-wing libertarianism, but also radical egalitarianism: they are both antithetical to individuality (one by means of coercion or marginalization, the other by means of extinguishing individuality altogether!)

(Here the notion of individuals can be extended to groups of individuals, cultures, and peoples. With this addendum the question naturally arises on the perils of ascribing “personhood” to collectives: the privileges of which must be checked at all times not to dwarf those of the individual.)
